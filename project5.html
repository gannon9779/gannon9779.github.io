Penn State Embroidery

The Nittany Lion mark looks elegant on merchandise—until you learn how much engineering hides inside “automatic” stitching.

Problem & Design

The project asked us to produce an embroidered piece that demonstrated control of the machine workflow: hooping, stabilizer choice, thread path planning, and software parameters that do not fight the artwork. I chose the Penn State logo because it is personally meaningful—I am a Penn Stater—and because it is deceptively intricate. The curves, negative space, and fine interior details reward patience and punish arrogance. I wanted something I would wear or display, not a generic test square.

From a design standpoint, the challenge was translating a familiar mark into thread physics. Embroidery is not “printing with string.” Stitch direction becomes texture. Density becomes drape. A decision that looks like a slider in software becomes a puckering problem in cotton. I approached the mark as a system of regions: outer silhouette, inner fills, high-risk corners where thread buildup could distort the face of the lion if I got greedy.

Brainstorming & Why I Created It

Early ideas bounced between minimalist line art (safer, less iconic) and full athletic marks (riskier, more “real”). I kept returning to Penn State because it connects my identity to the craft. This university is not just where I take classes; it is the community that shaped my adult habits—how I work in teams, how I handle pressure weeks, how I think about integrity in engineering. Putting that mark into thread felt like a honest use of the assignment’s freedom.

I also liked the logo as a technical object. It is culturally loaded, yes, but it is also a geometry puzzle. If you love intricacy, embroidery is a strange playground: you can spend an hour chasing a corner that most people will never notice—until it is wrong, and then everyone notices.

Machines / Tools Used

  • Embroidery machine (programmed stitch execution)
  • Palette software (file preparation and machine-side iteration)
  • Stabilizers, hoops, and thread appropriate to the fabric substrate

Struggles & Challenges Faced

I walked in assuming the machine would “just do it.” That assumption died quickly. Using the machine effectively takes time, effort, and real knowledge—thread tension gremlins, bobbin discipline, the emotional journey of watching a beautiful digital preview become a slightly tragic first physical pass. I hit limits I did not know existed. Some nights felt like debugging hardware without a clean stack trace: adjust, rerun, squint, adjust again.

Palette software became both lifeline and labyrinth. I spent serious hours learning how to be precise with parameters instead of hoping defaults would forgive me. I also wrestled with wiper-related errors—little failures that sound minor on paper and consume hours in reality. The project pushed me hard. It worked out in the end, but not because luck carried me; iteration carried me.

Instructor feedback

“Design is all about iterations and trials, and I think this project made you go through that. You've become a Palette software expert and "wiper-error" troubleshooter! Great work, Gannon!”

What This Project Taught Me

This project taught me to distrust the fantasy of “fully automated” fabrication. The machine automates motion; it does not automate judgment. I became better at reading failures as data—puckering means density, drift means hooping, weird gaps mean pathing. It also deepened my respect for craftspeople who make textiles look effortless. Finally, it gave me a story I can tell in interviews: I stayed with a hard process until the quality matched the meaning of the mark.
