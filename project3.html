Packaging & product form

Local Eye Packaging Box

A classroom packaging build grounded in a real summer system—when the printer bed is a hard requirement, the “vision” bends.

Problem & Design

Our class tackled packaging as a structured exercise: measure, sketch, prototype, and produce a physical box that could plausibly ship a product. Instead of inventing a fake gadget, I anchored the project in something I had actually been building—an offline smart doorbell system I have been calling Local Eye. That choice raised the stakes in a useful way. A toy brief lets you fudge dimensions; a project you care about forces you to decide what the box is supposed to communicate before you ever cut a flap. Is this “security?” Is it “privacy?” Is it “a device that refuses the cloud?” The form factor has to match the story.

I named the product deliberately. “Local” signals where the intelligence lives; “Eye” signals what the device does without sounding like a dystopian surveillance brand. The structural design problem was straightforward engineering drafting: develop a net, respect fold lines, verify clearances, and make sure the panels actually meet when taped or glued. The harder problem was translation: turning summer CAD brain into a cardboard reality that still looked intentional on a table in a classroom critique.

Brainstorming & Why I Created It

I could have built a generic “smart home device” box with safe copy and bland iconography. It would have graded fine. It also would have wasted a chance to integrate my Honors work with something I am already invested in. Local Eye is not just a label on cardboard for me; it is the physical edge of a larger argument about keeping doorbell intelligence local, auditable, and user-owned. Packaging became a practice arena for that narrative—how do you signal seriousness without pretending you are a Fortune 500 supply chain?

Brainstorming moved between minimal Japanese-style plain boxes (honest, but maybe too cold) and louder retail graphics (fun, but maybe too gimmicky for a privacy-first object). I landed in the middle: clean panels, strong name presence, room for future labeling work—because I already knew I wanted a second pass at professional labels and QR-linked transparency.

Machines / Tools Used

  • Cricut-based print/cut workflow (class constraints on bed size drove scale decisions)
  • Measuring tools and straightedges for verification
  • Cardboard / packaging stock available in the lab
  • Cutting and scoring implements appropriate to the mat workflow

Struggles & Challenges Faced

The first hard constraint was mechanical: the Cricut printer bed size capped how large each panel pattern could be, which forced the entire box to be down-scaled from what I originally wanted. That is a humbling moment—your “ideal” dimensions meet a bed dimension and lose. The right response is not denial; it is re-layout. I re-derived the net, re-checked fold tolerances, and accepted that packaging is always a negotiation with equipment.

The second constraint was aesthetic and material: I wanted black for a sleek, “security product” read, but only orange stock was realistically available in the window I had. I could have complained, or I could have shipped. I chose orange and leaned into crisp graphics so the color read as intentional rather than accidental. That is a lesson I will carry into engineering labs and future builds: you are often limited by what is on the shelf around you, and professionalism is what you do inside that boundary.

What This Project Taught Me

Packaging is structural engineering with branding on top. It also taught me to treat constraints as inputs, not insults—bed size and stock color reshaped the design, but they did not remove the identity of the product. Finally, it reinforced why I keep returning to Local Eye in this Honors sequence: physical objects are where abstract values become tangible. If privacy matters, it should survive contact with cardboard, tape, and the reality of a cutter bed.
