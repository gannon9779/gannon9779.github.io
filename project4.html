Branding & ethics

Local Eye Professional Labels

Product graphics as a promise—QR to GitHub, an eye mark you can read in half a second, and an asterisk that tells the truth loudly.

Problem & Design

Once the Local Eye box existed, it needed a face and a backstory you could read without opening a README. The design problem was intentionally hybrid: part brand identity, part regulatory-adjacent clarity, part ethical signaling. Consumer hardware often trains us to expect fine print that hides the uncomfortable truth. I wanted the opposite energy—language and layout that treat transparency as the product feature, not a liability to bury. That meant choosing a mark, hierarchy, and copy set that could survive a critique table and also survive my own standards if I imagine handing the box to someone I respect.

The logo direction converged on an eye symbol—immediate, slightly mythic, and legible at small scale. It represents what the device does (watches the door) without turning into a surveillance cliché if paired with the right words. Typography and spacing followed a simple rule: if a line matters morally, it should not look ashamed of itself. Whitespace is not emptiness; it is room for trust.

Brainstorming & Why I Created It

The fun center of this project was ideation around phrases—what Local Eye “sounds like” on a shelf. I chased versions that sounded too corporate, too hacker-y, too cute. The breakthrough was linking packaging to engineering openness: I decided to put the entire code base on GitHub and place a QR code on the back of the box. That is not just decoration; it is an argument in physical form. Scan this, verify me, fork me, criticize me—privacy and openness can coexist when you architect the system correctly.

The ironic asterisk choice came from watching real-world disclaimers manipulate attention. Usually the asterisk points to the bad news hidden at the bottom. I flipped the pattern: the asterisk draws your eye toward the sentence that says this device does not upload your information. The joke is gentle, but the point is not—hardware should not normalize extraction by default. If we are going to train users to read asterisks, we can train them to read good news with the same reflex.

Machines / Tools Used

  • Cricut workflow for producing printed label panels aligned to the packaging project
  • Design tooling for layout and typographic iteration (vector/raster prep consistent with the class pipeline)
  • Physical application to the Local Eye box (alignment, adhesion, and finish checks)

Struggles & Challenges Faced

The struggle was less about “can I print a label” and more about “can I stand behind every line of copy?” Product language is a kind of firmware. If you overclaim, you are lying; if you under-explain, you are hiding. Balancing clarity with the reality that this is still a student prototype—not a certified consumer security appliance—required restraint. The QR code helped because it moves the deep truth to a place where it can be long-form: the repository, the commits, the assumptions spelled out in text.

Visual integration also took iteration: aligning panels so seams do not cut through critical text, making sure the eye mark reads from an angle, and ensuring the QR has enough quiet zone to scan reliably. Those are small details, but they are the same species of details that separate a hobby mock from something you would show a recruiter as evidence of product thinking.

What This Project Taught Me

Branding is ethics in a different file format. This project taught me to treat packaging copy like an interface: every line should have a user-facing reason to exist. It also reinforced that openness is a design choice you can literalize—QR codes are cheap, shame is expensive. Finally, it sharpened my sense of irony as a tool: the asterisk joke only works if the engineering story underneath is serious. Humor without substance is just marketing; humor with substance is a mnemonic.
