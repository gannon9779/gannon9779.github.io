Honors Option — Building Meaning Through Making

View Projects

This portfolio is not a random collection of crafts. It is a thread of prototypes that all ask the same question in different ways: how do you take something that matters to you—identity, memory, loyalty, curiosity—and translate it into a physical object that still behaves like engineering work? I keep coming back to the Eagles and Penn State, to music that soundtracks long drives and childhood kitchens, to the trout that swim through the streams near State College, and to the quiet pride of building something my family can hold. Those anchors are not decoration; they are constraints that make the design problem real. When the subject is personal, you cannot hide behind vague “user personas.” You have to own the choices.

Across these projects, iteration stopped being an abstract buzzword and became a habit. The embroidery machine humbled me the same week the Cricut app tested my patience. Weeding tiny vinyl pieces for a Grateful Dead graphic taught me that “finished” is often just “good enough after the fourth pass.” Lithophanes and 3D prints reminded me that digital files are promises—what actually lands on the bed of the printer is where honesty shows up. I learned to love constraint: printer bed size forcing a packaging redesign, only orange stock available when I wanted black, a peg board that only works if every hole is where the math says it should be. Constraint is where design becomes engineering.

My workflow keeps moving between screen and shop. I live in Illustrator, Design Space, Canva, and the lithophane tools that bridge raster art into geometry the printer understands. That handoff—from pixels to toolpaths, from vectors to laser passes—is the same mental move as going from schematic to breadboard, just with different physics at the end. I care about how the file is built, whether paths are clean, whether tolerances survive the real machine, and whether the object still reads the way I intended once material grain and layer lines enter the picture.

Craftsmanship, for me, is what happens when troubleshooting becomes part of the aesthetic process. Embroidery turned me into someone who reads error codes and chases “wiper” issues in Palette software until the thread path behaves. Vinyl taught me that weeding is half design and half hand therapy. 3D printing onto fabric was pure experimentation: pause the job, slip the textile in, resume, and hope the layers bond the way the theory suggests. None of that is glamorous on a spreadsheet, but it is how prototypes become trustworthy.

Several pieces here orbit a summer project I call Local Eye—an offline smart doorbell concept built around privacy and local control. The packaging and labels became a place to practice product thinking: naming, visual identity, and the ethics of how we talk to users. I put the code on GitHub and added a QR code on the box so “openness” is not a slogan but a physical affordance. The ironic asterisk on the label—pointing to the sentence that says this device does not upload your information—flips the usual fine-print pattern. Transparency should be the headline, not the exception hidden at the bottom. That mindset runs alongside the rest of this work: build things that work, build things that mean something, and be willing to show your work.
