Laser & woodworking

Brown Trout Wooden Puzzle

A long build bridging memory, local ecology, and the gap between a perfect Illustrator file and a patient hand at the band saw.

Problem & Design

The core problem was to design a wooden puzzle that felt heirloom-adjacent: tight tolerances, satisfying piece shapes, a silhouette that reads from across a room, and a fabrication plan that respected both laser capabilities and traditional wood cutting. I chose a brown trout because it is famous in the State College area in a way that locals understand without explanation—cold water, limestone streams, the quiet obsession of people who wake up early for fish that do not care about your schedule. The trout is personal, too: it ties me to place, to hiking trails where water sound replaces screen glow, to a culture of conservation and patience.

Digitally, the puzzle is a graph problem disguised as art: islands of pieces, interlocks that cannot be too fragile, kerf allowances that have to survive real laser width, and negative space that still communicates fins and speckles without becoming splinters waiting to happen. Physically, it is a woodworking project: stock selection, saw discipline, sanding strategy, and the slow work of making edges feel intentional instead of accidental.

Brainstorming & Why I Created It

The emotional seed was a wooden shark puzzle my grandfather made for me when I was a kid. I can still remember the weight of the pieces in my hand—the slight burn smell from laser-cut edges, the way wood warmth feels different from plastic toys. I did not want to copy his work; I wanted to inherit its spirit: an animal form, a puzzle logic, a reason to spend hours in a shop because love is sometimes expressed as tolerances.

Brainstorming moved through other species and shapes, but the trout kept winning because it anchored the object in my current geography. Penn State is landlocked in the cultural imagination, but the center of Pennsylvania is not empty of water culture—it is rich with it. I wanted the object to feel like a souvenir of attention: if you notice streams, you notice the state differently.

Machines / Tools Used

  • Adobe Illustrator (geometry, puzzle seams, iteration on paths)
  • Laser engraver / cutter (cutting puzzle profiles and detail)
  • Band saw (rough breakdown and shaping as the workflow required)
  • Additional wood cutting tools for trimming, cleanup, and finishing passes
  • Sanding and finishing supplies appropriate to the chosen stock

Struggles & Challenges Faced

This was a long project in calendar time, and Illustrator ate hours the way long homework sets do—you adjust a curve that looked fine yesterday, discover a collision between two piece boundaries, zoom until your eyes hurt, then discover the laser has opinions about your opinions. The digital-to-physical handshake is unforgiving. A tiny gap error becomes a wobble in the hand. A too-bold interior cut becomes a break risk when a kid (or an adult who acts like a kid) pulls pieces apart.

Woodworking added the second layer of difficulty: power tools reward respect and punish hurry. I spent long stretches at the band saw and other cutting stations cleaning up profiles, reminding myself that “almost straight” is not straight when pieces mate. Sanding is not glamorous, but it is where the object becomes pleasant to touch. The struggles stacked, but each pass taught me something concrete about how files become furniture-adjacent objects.

What This Project Taught Me

This project taught me that patience is a manufacturing input. It reinforced my belief that digital precision is only half the story—the other half is tactile QA and respect for material. It also gave me a personal anchor: prototyping can carry memory without being sentimental in a cheap way. The trout is not only “cool wood art”; it is place, family craft lineage, and engineering iteration sharing one object. I am proud of the final result because it earned itself.
