About

I am an electrical engineering student who keeps one foot in equations and the other in the fab lab. I built this site as part of my Schreyer Honors creative prototyping option—a place to document not just what I made, but how I thought while I made it.

I care about objects that are technically defensible and personally honest. A prototype can be “correct” on paper and still feel hollow if the story behind it is thin. I tend to chase projects where those two demands overlap: packaging for a privacy-minded doorbell concept, a trout puzzle rooted in local ecology and family memory, a lithophane light box built from real childhood photographs. Those projects force me to practice product thinking—materials, narrative, failure modes—without pretending I am shipping at industrial scale.

Fabrication is where I learn fastest. Iteration is not a slide in a deck; it is re-cutting vinyl, re-truing a laser file, re-threading a machine, or walking back to the printer with a slicer setting I swore I would not touch again. I like the loop: sketch, model, cut, print, test, adjust. I also like the humility that loop enforces. The machines do not care about my timeline.

Several entries in this portfolio connect to Local Eye, an offline smart doorbell system I developed over the summer. The hardware and software story lives elsewhere; here, you will see how I carried that work into branding exercises—physical boxes, labels, QR codes to GitHub, and language that treats privacy as a first-class design requirement rather than an afterthought.

If you are here from recruiting: I am interested in roles where building, testing, and communicating about technology all matter—hardware-adjacent software, prototyping, or product development with a maker’s respect for the details. If you are here from Honors: thank you for reading closely. This portfolio is my attempt to prove that creative work and engineering discipline can share the same table.
