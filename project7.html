Wood & precision layout

Cracker Barrel Peg Game Base

After a trout-shaped marathon, a triangle of holes felt simple—until I remembered simplicity is where mistakes show first.

Problem & Design

The design goal was straightforward: fabricate a wooden base that supports the classic Cracker Barrel-style triangle tee peg game—fifteen holes in a tight lattice, consistent depth, clean spacing, and a board that sits flat without rocking. Straightforward does not mean trivial. A puzzle trout forgives a little romance; a game board does not forgive a hole that is off by an eighth. Players notice bias immediately. If holes wander, the pegs bind or the geometry feels “wrong” even if people cannot articulate why.

I treated the triangle as a small measurement exercise: establish reference edges, lay out points from a consistent baseline, and drill with discipline so each hole is perpendicular and uniform. The aesthetic goal was quiet craftsmanship—something you would actually leave on a coffee table, not a rough lab prototype that hides in a closet.

Brainstorming & Why I Created It

Nostalgia drove the idea. Cracker Barrel peg boards are part road-trip ritual, part low-stakes competition, part tactile fidget. I grew up playing that game while waiting for pancakes and country-fried whatever. Making my own base felt like reclaiming a small piece of childhood and proving I could execute a “boring” geometry with non-boring care. After the trout puzzle’s complexity, I wanted a project that emphasized fundamentals: measure twice, drill once, sand like you mean it.

Brainstorming stayed intentionally narrow. I did not need a reinvented game; I needed a clean execution of a known rule set. That restraint was its own creative choice—confidence expressed as precision rather than novelty.

Machines / Tools Used

  • Wood stock selected for flatness and ease of finishing
  • Measuring tools and layout square for point placement
  • Drill and drill bits sized to the pegs used in the game
  • Sanding tools for edge break and surface cleanup

Struggles & Challenges Faced

The main challenge was maintaining uniformity: keeping hole spacing consistent across rows, preventing wander as the bit enters, and avoiding tear-out on the exit side of the board. Small errors accumulate visually in a triangle pattern because your eye compares symmetric neighbors. I slowed down, used piloting discipline where appropriate, and checked progress against the layout instead of assuming my hand stayed true.

Compared to the trout puzzle, this build consumed less total time, which was intentional—but the shorter schedule does not mean lower standards. If anything, shorter projects tempt rushed layout. I had to consciously treat “simple” as “easy to verify,” not “easy to wing.”

What This Project Taught Me

This project taught me that fundamentals are a portfolio skill, not a beginner phase. Recruiters and professors both notice clean holes. It also reinforced that nostalgia can be a valid design brief if you execute with adult rigor. Finally, it gave me a mental palate cleanser: not every build has to be maximalist to be meaningful—sometimes engineering beauty is just symmetry done honestly.
