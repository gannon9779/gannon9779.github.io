Print & graphics

Cricut Stickers

When “any design is possible” becomes its own design problem—three stickers, three stories, one playoff-season headspace.

Problem & Design

The assignment was deceptively open: use the Cricut workflow alongside a color printer to produce stickers where the graphic possibilities are basically unlimited. Unlimited sounds freeing until you sit down and realize it is another word for “no compass.” I had to define my own brief. The design goal became less about technical novelty and more about curation: could I pick three images that each carried enough personal weight to justify the ink, the cut line, and the time in the app? I wanted stickers I would actually place on a laptop or water bottle—not filler graphics I would peel off a week later.

From a fabrication standpoint, the problem split in two halves. First, file hygiene: raster versus vector decisions, bleed, and making sure the printer and cutter were not fighting each other on registration. Second, narrative hygiene: each sticker needed to read quickly at a glance while still holding a longer story if someone asked about it. That balance—iconic silhouette versus emotional depth—is the same tension you see in product icons and packaging marks, just at a smaller scale.

Brainstorming & Why I Created It

I started the way you are supposed to start: by listing things I love and letting the list get embarrassingly long. Sports, travel dreams, music, places, phrases that stuck in my head from random Wikipedia rabbit holes. The Eagles were alive in the culture again—playoff energy, old-school logo nostalgia, that specific Philadelphia blend of pride and chaos. I leaned into the old-school Eagles mark because it is more than a brand to me; it is Saturdays with friends, radio call volume turned up, and the feeling of a city that expects you to show up with your whole personality. The timing mattered. The logo had been brought back into the conversation, and I wanted my object to feel like it belonged to that moment, not a generic football clip art sheet.

The second sticker came from a quieter place: I have been seriously researching a study abroad decision focused on South Korea. While reading, I kept noticing descriptions of a communal mindset—how people talk about persistence, social harmony, and showing up consistently for the people next to you. I do not want to flatten an entire culture into a sticker slogan, so I treated this one like a personal mnemonic: something that reminds me why I am doing the paperwork, the language practice, and the awkward budgeting spreadsheets. It is a study-abroad sticker that is really a commitment device.

The third sticker is pure memory lane: Willie Nelson’s “On the Road Again.” I listened to that song constantly as a kid, usually in the background of family trips where the destination mattered less than the motion. As I get older, the lyric feels less like tourism and more like a philosophy—always searching for the next chapter, the next version of life, the next place where I will have to reintroduce myself. I wanted that one to feel warm, a little worn-in, like a jukebox selection rather than a corporate sticker pack.

Machines / Tools Used

The build stayed close to the tools we actually had in the workflow:

  • Cricut cutting machine
  • Cricut Design Space (layout, cut preparation)
  • Color printer (full-color sticker prints)
  • Sticker media appropriate for print-and-cut

Struggles & Challenges Faced

The Cricut app is powerful and also uniquely gifted at making you feel like you are one mis-tap away from ruining an hour of setup. Small interface friction stacks: aligning mats, double-checking mirror settings, wondering if the machine will respect the bleed you think you assigned. None of it is impossible, but it is the kind of cognitive load that shows up in real prototyping—where the tool is not “hard,” it is just dense.

The bigger struggle was self-inflicted: too many ideas and not enough constraints early on. When any design is possible, you oscillate. You fall in love with five directions at once. You almost start a sixth because social media brain says novelty equals value. I had to force a decision architecture: three stickers, three reasons, no “just one more” slot. That constraint turned out to be the real design move. The stickers got better the moment I stopped treating infinity like a feature.

What This Project Taught Me

This project taught me that creativity without boundaries often stalls, while creativity with boundaries ships. It also reinforced something I keep telling myself as an engineer: the emotional brief is part of the system requirements. If I cannot explain why a graphic belongs on my own laptop, I should not expect a stranger to care. Finally, it was a good reminder that software ergonomics matter as much as hardware—Design Space is part of the machine, and respecting its quirks is part of professional patience.
